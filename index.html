Introduction

Lasagna has many versions, both within Italy and around the world, but when we talk about “real” lasagna in Italy we mean “Lasagna alla Bolognese” i.e., from Bologna in the region of Emilia-Romagna. One of the traditional pasta shapes of this region is the long, flat strips that go into making the layers of lasagna. Although some Italians will buy these strips dried, it’s still quite common in Emilia-Romagna to make them at home, along with other regional specialties like tortellini.

Fresh sheets of egg pasta always taste better in lasagna than dried ones. Here in Italy we are lucky enough to even be able to buy high-quality, handmade pasta sheets fresh from the Pastificio or pasta shop.



Ingredients

Besciamella ingredients (white sauce)

  • 100g Salted Butter
  • 80g Flour
  • 1l Whole Milk
  • Pinch of Salt
  • Nutmeg

Ragù ingredients

  • 250g Ground pork (very fat)
  • 500g Ground beef
  • 50g Carrots
  • 50g Onions
  • 50g Celery
  • 40ml Whole milk
  • 1 tablespoon Extravirgin Olive Oil
  • Salt
  • Black Pepper
  • 250g Cooking White Wine
  • 3l Water

And of course

  • 21pcs Lasagna pasta sheets


Step by step

Ragù Sauce

1 2 3

Heat a healthy glug of olive oil in a large pan while you peel the carrot and the onion. Wash and dry the celery. Cut the three of them into tiny pieces. and cook them over a low heat until they are soft (roughly 5 minutes).

Turn the heat to medium-high and add the ground beef and ground pork. When the meat has browned and the liquid it releases has evaporated, add the white wine and stir.

When the wine has evaporated, add the passata (tomato sauce), salt, and pepper. Stir again to mix the ingredients.
4 5 6

Add the first liter of water, stir all the ingredients and let it cook for an hour.

After 1 hour, add another liter of water and 40ml of whole milk. Cook for another hour.

After that, add the last liter of water and cook for another hour. The longer a ragù cooks, the tastier it becomes but three hours is enough. Remember to keep the heat as low as you can (and still have the occasional small bubble rising to the surface) and to stir from time to time. Tasting it is the best way to decide if the ragù is ready!

Besciamella Sauce

1 2 3

Warm the milk in a saucepan, but don’t let it boil.

In a separate saucepan, cube the butter and melt it over low heat. As soon as it has liquefied take it off the heat and gradually whisk in the flour, making sure that no lumps form.

Place the pan with the butter and flour mixture back on a low heat and add your warm milk, salt, nutmeg and ground pepper. Stir rapidly with the whisk until it thickens and becomes smooth. When making a bechamel sauce, don’t stop stirring until you have added and incorporated all of your ingredients – this will help you to keep lumps from forming

Time to put your lasagna layers together!

1 2 3

Pour a fine layer of besciamella on the bottom of your casserole dish.

Cover it with a layer of ragù.

Place your pasta rectangles side-by-side on top of the ragu until you have covered it completely. Don’t allow them to overlap too much – you don’t want any double layers of pasta.
4 5 6

Repeat the process in the same order as above until you reach 5 or 6 layers of pasta. On the very last layer of pasta place a slightly thicker layer of bechamel and ragù as it is the layer most exposed to the hot air of the oven and tends to dry up a little. Sprinkle some parmesan cheese on top, or between the layers if you like, but this is not a requirement.

Place the casserole in a pre-heated oven at 200° Celsius (390° Fahrenheit) for about 25 minutes. You will know the lasagna is ready when you can see a golden crust around the outside edges on the top layer.

Let your gorgeous lasagna rest for a couple of minutes before cutting and serving. Enjoy!
